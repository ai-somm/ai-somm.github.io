Trip Report

Alsace | Baden | Pfalz

We made our cross-Atlantic trip to Alsace in December 2022, with clear intentions of visiting as many domaines in Alsace as we could, wandering around some Christmas markets, and perhaps hitting some weinguts in Baden and Pfalz in passing.

We flew Lufthansa from SFO to FRA and took the Lufthansa express bus down to Strasbourg. The next morning we rented a car and started driving south and visiting domaines along the way. We stayed in Ribeauville for six nights as our base for winery visits in Alsace and Baden. We then drove up to Deidesheim as our base for winery visits in Pfalz.

Lessons Learnt - Trip Logistics
  • Almost all Alsacien wineries require reservations in advance. How far in advance depends on its popularity. For example, Zind-Humbrecht requires 6-8 weeks in advance, and as does Domaine Weinbach. But most other wineries can be scheduled with a few days' or just one day's notice.
  • Many Pfalz wineries with tasting rooms welcome walk-ins and do not take reservations. Smaller wineries do require reservations like Alsacien wineries, but most can be scheduled promptly.
  • Wine suitcase (mine is VinGardeValise bought at $369) is probably the best solution so far for taking a case or two back home (to US) with us. Haus Packs (inflatable air columns as cushioning) with air pumps are the best wine sleeves for wrapping small numbers of bottles.
  • Direct shipping with Cote d'Or Imports (with which Marcel Deiss works) or MBE (with which Marc Tempe works) turns out to be our choice for shipping cases of wines back home. A case runs about $190 but you don't have to pay taxes (which you would pay if you were to go with wine suitcases as checked luggage.).
  • Tasting at five domaines per day sounds ambitious but is totally feasible if planned meticulously :)
  • DO make dinner reservations for restaurants! Restaurants open at very restricted hours and are always fully booked. We learnt it the hard way.
Lessons Learnt - Wine Study
  • Many Alsacien producers have been experimenting Syrah or Shiraz in light of global warming with encouraging results. None released yet, but may well be in a few years.
  • Almost every Alsacien producer makes skin contact (orange) wines off Pinot Gris or Gewurztraminer.
  • Alsace, Pfalz, and Baden have been the overlooked, underrated, and best value premier Pinor Noir producing regions for quite a while now.
  • Klevener de Heiligenstein: pink-skinned Savagnin Rose variety that's been planted and vinified by very few domaines left. Usually with some skin-contact or in an oxidative style.
  • Pinot Blanc and Auxerrois: as is well known these two are usually blended. One could make a 100% Auxerrois cuvee and label it as Pinot Blanc, but not the other way around (though nor would anyone do such thing...).
  • After this trip of 28 wineries (domaines, weinguts) tasting through a few hundreds of wines including various expressions of the same grand cru sites, I finally got the hang of how to taste soil types or lieux-dix in wines... the crushed wet stone kinda minerality in Rangen, the salty minerality in Schlossberg, and the tension and smoke in Kastelberg...
  • All the Alsacien producers we tasted at swear by selection massale off Burgundy scions offered by local nurseries (e.g. Hebinger), and are under constant stress of mildews, frosts, and esca pressures with nepoviruses also an issue in Pfalz, whereas Pfalz and Baden producers we tasted at use a mix of German clones and Burgundy clones like Dijon for Pinot Noir.
  • Being the mosaic landscape of soil compositions in Alsace, every grand cru (now 51, soon to be 53) is unique in its own way. However, sandstone appears to be everywhere, volcanic soils appears to be the most characterful and distinctive in terms of manifestation in wines, such as Rangen (volcanic) and Kastelberg (slate). And the same, I'd argue, goes with Forst (volcanic) in Pfalz.
  • Two new grand cru sites just got approved on May 13, 2022 for Pinot Noir: Kirchsberg de Barr (limestone), Hengst (limestone-marl). They will go into effect in 2023.
  • We learnt about various changes happeninng at different domaines to hedge against global warming: from starting to blend warmer sites with cooler sites (Bechtold) to experimenting with alternative grape varieties such as Syrah and Shiraz (JosMeyer, Bott-Geyl, Meyer Fonne, etc.), from using coal as compost material to increase soil water holding capacity (Koehler-Ruprecht) to pushing for biodynamic and/or regenerative viticulture to improve the resilience of vines (Lissner, Bechtold, Zind-Humbrecht, Schoffit, etc. too many.), from experimenting with echalas for vine training (Marc Kreydenweiss) to tailoring Poulssard, simple or double Guyot for each vine (JosMeyer, Weinbach, etc.). Global warming is clearly on every grower's mind and everyone is actively seeking solutions.

Domaine Bechtold
  • Organic and biodynamic. The Bechtold family in the neat little town of Marleheim 20 minutes west of Strasbourg off Route du Vin, vinifies Riesling, Muscat, Gewurztraminer, and Cremant in stainless steel tanks and Pinor Noir, Pinot Meunier in 4th fill pieces from Burgundy. As part of Neal Rosenthal's portfolio, they are visited by Neal every January, when Neal tastes all the current releases and decides on which ones and how many to import. So far, Neal has only been taking dry wines from them. One interesting bit about how Neal took skin-contact Gewurztraminer: when he tasted it, he was really impressed but had to seek confirmation so he called Jeremy immediately and got off the phone with a 900-bottle deal.
  • The tasting room is clearly just a room in their family home retrofitted to include a tasting counter. The house cat -- a tripod orange tabby GIRL with only one paw happily roamed around and greeted us. Mrs. Bechtold warmly received us and she told us that her son has taken over the winemaking and is vacationing to return around Xmas. Interestingly at our tasting at Marc Kreydenweiss that followed immediately after, we learnt that the current winemaker interned with Marc Kreydenweiss and the two domaines have been very close with one another.
  • Vintage 2018 was a great year with a dry spring, and really nice weathers during summer -- dry, and not too hot.
  • Vintage 2020 was a hot year, leading to aromatic whites at the domaine.
  • Vintage 2021 was a rather difficult vintage. It was raining all summer, which led to lots of mildews. The family tried their best to keep mildrews under control but still lost 40% yield, which has never happened in the past 40 years. To combat mildews, they resorted to the biodynamic measures: using sting nettles, prele??, picking when the sap flow is at the lowest, spraying tea essence, and the cream of grass and tea, etc.

Marc Kreydenweiss
  • Perhaps best known for their GC Kastelberg bottling on the powerful black schist soils. Marc Kreydenweiss was helmed by the Marc family since 1984. Low yielding, low intervention in celler, and focused on terrior expressions, 1985 marked the full conversion to biodynamic viticulture. In 1990, the family purchased a winery in Languedoc, which is now managed by Jean. In 2007, Antoine took over winemaking in the Alsace/Andlau domaine. All wines in Alsace are vinified in the traditional foudres, and all wines in Languedoc are vinified in stainless steel and concrete tanks.
  • A small experimental plot of vines are trained high on single stalk (echalas), like those commonly seen in northern Rhone.
  • Their Pinot Noir is made with short maceration, no new oak, no cold soak, 100% whole cluster, with destemming only used in difficult vintages.
  • Vintage 2020 was warm and dry with healthy fruit thus easy harvest. Wines have the profile of cooler vintages such as 2019 but with even better freshness. Harvest usually starts early mid September, and lasts until early to mid October.
  • Each year an artist is commissioned to create label arts.
  • Two US importers: AP Imports and Williams Connor Wine in Virginia.

Kientzler
  • Kientzler makes such beautifully terrior-focused wines. On their labels, only grand cru terriors are featured whereas grape varieties fade into the background as a tiny letter print. For instance, an R enclosed by a minimalist square represents Riesling, as does G Gewurztraminer, or M Muscat, etc. All stainless steel vinification to let the terrior shine. All wines are bottled the year after, and released after years aged in bottle. This is one of the domaines that showcases soils and terriors the most crystal clear. Their Riesling GC Kirchberg smells and tastes much more similar to their Muscat or Pinot Gris GC Kirchberg than their Riesling GC Geisberg or lieu-dit Muhlfost.
  • Vintage 2019 resulted in more concentrated wines at low yield at 40 hl/ha.
  • Geisberg is a very challenging terrior featuring very steep slopes on limestone and sandstone. Odium, esca, and mildews are common threats, and trunks are renewed (recépage) to combat these fungal disease. Kientzler produces 3000-6000 bottles every vintage. Compared to Muscat (messier cluster than Riesling), Riesling clusters are tighter.
  • 50% exported. 30 hectares that come out between 50k and 70k bottles per vintage. US importer: AP Imports.

♥ Domaine Marcel Deiss ♥
  • Marcel Deiss needs no introduction. Best known for their coplanted ancient vines that go into co-fermented cuvees that put emphases on terrior expressions rather varietal characters. Vines ages range between 80-85 years old. Coplanted vines are all harvested at the same time. Massale selection in practice.
  • For instance, in Marcel Deiss's GC Altenberg plot, there are over 85 different varieties (of any color), at least 3-5 massale selected Riesling varieties. And their oldest vines are in GC Schoenenberg.
  • Not all its neighbors buy such a philosophy. For instance, when we were visiting Domaine Weinbach the next day tasting their newest cuvee that is a blend of Pinot Gris and Gewurztraminer of their newly acquired parcels. Theo Faller specifically told us that they don't think the coplantation works well as different varieties ripen at different times and are suited to different terriors.
  • Vintage 2019 marks generous amount of sunshine that resulted in fruits with ripe acidity, and a healthy balance.
  • Vintage 2021 was the most complicated vintage in the last 50 years and Marcel Deiss also lost 60% yield to rains that caused mildews.
  • Mambourg - limestone that results in wide acidity; Schlossberg - granite that results in vertical wines; Altenberg - clay and limestone, also the only GC in Alsace that allows all 13 varieties; Schoenenerg - clay and gypse (gypsum); Zotzenberg is the only GC that allows Sylvaner; Hengst and Kirchberg de Barr are two GCs for Pinot Noir; Kaefferkopt allows 60-80% Gewurztraminer, 10-40% Riesling, <30% Pinot Gris, <10% Muscat.
  • Biodynamic since decades ago. They use pied de cuve for stuck fermentation.

Bott-Geyl
  • Created in 1775 by the marriage of familie Bott and familie Geyl. 99% production is white wines, with one red white and two sparkling wines.
  • Produces from 6 varieties (Riesling, Muscat, Gewurztraminer, Pinot Noir, Pinot Blanc, Pinor Gris) and 6 grand cru sites (Schlossberg, Schonenberg, Mandelberg, Sporen, Furstentum, Snnenglanz).
  • Biodynamic since 2000 - one of the first five growers to convert, along with Z-H, Marcel Deiss, Vicent Flheit.
  • Long aging in bottle before release. For instance, current release of Pinot Gris GC is 2012. 90k bottles produced per year on average, but around 350k bottles are held in the cellar for extended aging.
  • Vifinication is done partly in stainless steel tanks and foudres (half and half). Foudres at the domaine come in all sizes according to the size of the parcels. During the fermentation, vessels are swapped between stainless steel and used oak barrels to keep wines in balance. For instance, if the acidity is too high, the wine would be racked into foudres to round it out.
  • Bott-Geyl aims to make wines of terrior and emotions. Organic and biodynamic farming is practiced. Natural sulphr or copper sprays are used for mildews. No chemical products used whatsoever. Sulphur usage is also limited --- a bit in tank after pressing before kicking off fermentation to prevent microbial spoilage.
  • Has been experimenting with Syrah in the past few vintages, as Vin de France
  • Export 70%.

  • To quote from Skurnik website: Scheurebe (Sh-oy-Rey-Buh) is an oft-misunderstood and wonderful grape variety. A cross of Riesling and Bukettraube (Silvaner x Trollinger), Scheurebe is highly aromatic with bracing, Riesling-like acidity. In the right hands, it can produce wines in a full spectrum of styles, from dry to sweet, expressing soil and place clearly. It’s also a variety that ages beautifully --- 1989 Müller-Catoir Scheurebe Auslese is a legendary wine and many excellent versions from the 1970s and 1980s can be sampled at Kruger-Rumpf in the Nahe. Scheu (rhymes with “joy”) might remind you of Sauvignon Blanc in its aroma, without the green, grassy, or bell peppery-pyrazine character. It’s frequently associated with aromas of grapefruit and gooseberry with a spicy, peppery note. These esters and citrusy aldehydes are often accompanied by a wild, exotic, and somewhat “animal” terpene aroma, found in guava and passionfruit, especially when Scheu is harvested very ripe. The intense aromatics of the variety are balanced by juiciness and acid-driven structure that gives way to more woodsy and non-fruit flavors as the wine moves towards the finish. “I love this variety most when it has one foot in on the fruit side and one foot in the wild, musky side,” says Georg Rumpf, whose Scheurebe, planted in the Grand Cru of Dautenpflänzer, may be the oldest in Germany.
